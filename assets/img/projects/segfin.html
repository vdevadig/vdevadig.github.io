Clustering Techniques Used:¶

K-Means Clustering:¶

K-Means Clustering is a 'Centroid based' Unsupervised Learning Algorithm and its main task is to divide the datapoints we have to internally cohesive and mutually distinct clusters.

Methodology:

- Choose the desired number of clusters (k) or use the elbow method to identify the optimal number of clusters. Randomly select k data points as initial cluster centroids.

- Calculate the distance between each data point and all centroids. Assign each data point to the cluster with the nearest centroid. Iteratively performed to recompute centroids for all clusters.

Fun example, imagine we are at a party and we need to group ourselves based on our unique quirks, (Mine is random nose flaring, whats yours?). K-means is a party host, but a slightly mischievous one at that. Three random people are chosen and declared them the "group leaders." Now, everyone has to join the leader nearest them, forming three initial groups. But here comes a twist! The leaders themselves are changed based on who joins their group, becoming the average of everyone around them. So, someone with a hair-eating habit might end up leading one of the groups.

The whole charade repeats, with people switching groups if the new leader is a better fit.

In the end, you have three (or however many the host chooses) distinct groups based on their "weird habits." K-means does the same thing with data, but instead of thumb-sucking, it uses things like purchase history and website clicks. It helps businesses understand their customers better, like figuring out who buys the most pickles (and why!).

So, the next time you see a product recommendation that's oddly specific, remember, it might just be the mischievous hand of K-means at work, playing its party game with your data!

Hierarchical Clustering:¶

Hierarchical Clustering is a tree-based unsupervised learning algorithm to create a hierarchy of clusters, organizing data points from individual elements to a single cluster encompassing everything.

Methodology:

  • Each data point representing a customer is initially a separate cluster.

  • Based on Euclidian Distances, a linkage matrix is generated.

  • Ward linkage considers both inter-cluster distance (between clusters) and intra-cluster variance.

  • Merging minimizes the increase in both, ensuring newly formed clusters remain cohesive.

  • These two clusters merge into a single larger cluster.

  • The linkage matrix and internal representations of merged clusters are updated accordingly.

  • This iterative merging process continues until all data points belong to a single cluster. The merging history is visualized as a dendrogram, a tree-like structure depicting the hierarchical relationships between clusters at different merging stages.

Another cool example, imagine a party where everyone's a data point. Hierarchical clustering is like finding friend groups based on how people naturally mingle. Each data point is its own "friend group", we then look for data points most similar to each other (like two people sharing inside jokes). We merge them into one "friend group."

We repeat this until everyone's connected. We go on finding and merging the closest groups, forming larger and larger groups.

We finally stop at the "cool kids" table, as we don't want everyone in one giant group, so we choose a level in the "friend group tree" (dendrogram) that represents the desired number of clusters.

Now, these clusters are like different customer segments in marketing. You can target specific groups with tailored campaigns, just like how jokes at the "cool kids" table might not resonate with the "bookworm corner."
